### Strength figures: the documented disagreement set
- **Longstreet (1896, p. 315, verbatim):** "only four thousand seven hundred under Pickett were fresh; the entire force of these divisions was only fifteen thousand strong… manned by thrice their numbers… lost about sixty per cent."
- **Busey & Martin** (via verified secondary reproductions — the book is not free): Pickett's division **~5,820 engaged**; column totals reproduced at **11,484–12,500**.
- **Hess 2001:** column **~12,000–12,500**. In-copyright (UNC Press; archive.org copy is controlled-lending only) — citable via library access, no free URL. *(A claim about where its strength tables sit internally was refuted 0-3 — unknown.)*
- **Stewart 1957:** ~**10,500** (via Encyclopedia Virginia's historiography summary).
- ⚠️ **Refuted 0-3, do not use:** "Longstreet says ~13,000 at p. 386." Not in the text.
- **Most contested:** Pettigrew's and Trimble's July 3 engaged strengths, because of July 1 losses. The per-brigade extraction from OR returns + B&M reproductions remains to be done (authoring-prep task).

### Brigade-level structure (now multiply attested)
Longstreet pp. 388–389 (verified verbatim) gives the assault organization: Pickett right (Kemper, Garnett, supported by Armistead); Wilcox in echelon on Pickett's right; Pettigrew's division on Pickett's left (Archer's brigade under **Fry**, Pettigrew's [Marshall's — editorial gloss, Longstreet writes only "Pettigrew's"], Brockenbrough, Davis); Scales's brigade under **Lowrance** + Lane, both under Trimble. Note: Lang's Floridians are *not* named in this passage. Rawley Martin adds Pickett's division regiment-by-regiment: Kemper right (1st, 3rd, 7th, 11th, 24th VA), Garnett left (8th, 18th, 19th, 28th, 56th VA), Armistead rear-center (9th, 14th, 38th, 53rd, 57th VA) — with the flag that some modern accounts place Armistead left-rear behind Garnett rather than Martin's "rear and center."

## Gap B — Four new verified voices (each with free full text + verbatim-checked quotes)

**Rawley W. Martin** (Lt. Col., 53rd VA, Armistead's brigade) + **John Holmes Smith** (Capt., 11th VA, Kemper's) — SHSP Vol. 32 (1904), pp. 183–195; Martin's letter dated Aug. 11, 1897. Full text: https://archive.org/stream/southernhistoricv32sout/southernhistoricv32sout_djvu.txt (mirror: gdg.org — expired TLS, use http). **The Armistead vignette:** hat on sword point, he "rushed forward, scaled the wall, and cried: 'Boys, give them the cold steel!'" and "had fallen, mortally wounded, under the guns he had captured." Martin: "I was disabled at Armistead's side a moment after he had fallen." Also corroborates pass-1 timing from inside the column: "About 1 o'clock two signal guns were fired by the Washington Artillery." Flags: 34-year recollection; twice misnames the Union position "Seminary Ridge."

**Birkett D. Fry** (Col., commanding Archer's brigade) — "Pettigrew's Charge at Gettysburg," SHSP Vol. 7 (1879), pp. 91–93. Full text: http://www.gdg.org/research/SHSP/shpettig.html (cite an archive.org SHSP v7 scan as backup). His brigade was the **guide of the whole assault** ("in the assault both divisions should allign themselves by it" — Garnett agreed to "dress on my command"). **Disagreement flag, encode it:** against Virginia-centric accounts (and Haskell), Fry insists Pettigrew's men fought to the wall — "All of the five regimental colors of my command reached the line of the enemy's works" — while Brockenbrough's brigade genuinely broke early; modern scholarship partially supports both. Caveats: Fry was down wounded before the end; misdates Heth's wounding.

**James Longstreet** — *From Manassas to Appomattox* (1896). Full texts: https://archive.org/details/manassasappomatt00longrich and Gutenberg #38418. The fifteen-thousand protest (pp. 386–387) is **indirect discourse in this memoir** — "Opinion was then expressed that the fifteen thousand men who could make successful assault over that field had never been arrayed for battle" — the famous direct-speech version comes from his earlier 1877 account; don't conflate. The order-refusal moment (p. 392): "Pickett said, 'General, shall I advance?' The effort to speak the order failed, and I could only indicate it by an affirmative bow." His reproduction of Alexander's 1:40 note differs slightly in wording from Alexander's own 1907 version — flag, don't reconcile.

**Ralph Orson Sturtevant** (13th Vermont, Stannard's brigade) — *Pictorial History Thirteenth Regiment Vermont Volunteers* (1910), pp. 304–305. Full text: https://archive.org/details/cu31924030916187. **The flank-attack vignette:** Stannard's "famous and now historic order (unique in manoeuvring in the midst of battle) 'Change front, forward on first company,'" after which each company "saw in their immediate front not sixty yards away General Pickett's command… and at once opened a deadly fire on their flank." Flag: the 1910 history ignores the known Stannard-vs-Doubleday/Hancock credit dispute.

**Still missing (2 of 6):** Frederick Fuger on Cushing's death (would resolve/document the head-vs-mouth wound conflict with Haskell — likely in his 1907 Journal of the Military Service Institution article), and a Union enlisted bombardment voice (19th MA — Waitt 1906 — or 1st MN history). Targeted single-source lookups, not another broad pass.
