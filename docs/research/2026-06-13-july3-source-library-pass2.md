### Strength figures: the documented disagreement set
- **Longstreet (1896, p. 315, verbatim):** "only four thousand seven hundred under Pickett were fresh; the entire force of these divisions was only fifteen thousand strong… manned by thrice their numbers… lost about sixty per cent."
- **Busey & Martin** (via verified secondary reproductions — the book is not free): Pickett's division **~5,820 engaged**; column totals reproduced at **11,484–12,500**.
- **Hess 2001:** column **~12,000–12,500**. In-copyright (UNC Press; archive.org copy is controlled-lending only) — citable via library access, no free URL. *(A claim about where its strength tables sit internally was refuted 0-3 — unknown.)*
- **Stewart 1957:** ~**10,500** (via Encyclopedia Virginia's historiography summary).
- ⚠️ **Refuted 0-3, do not use:** "Longstreet says ~13,000 at p. 386." Not in the text.
- **Most contested:** Pettigrew's and Trimble's July 3 engaged strengths, because of July 1 losses. The per-brigade extraction from OR returns + B&M reproductions remains to be done (authoring-prep task).

### Brigade-level structure (now multiply attested)
Longstreet pp. 388–389 (verified verbatim) gives the assault organization: Pickett right (Kemper, Garnett, supported by Armistead); Wilcox in echelon on Pickett's right; Pettigrew's division on Pickett's left (Archer's brigade under **Fry**, Pettigrew's [Marshall's — editorial gloss, Longstreet writes only "Pettigrew's"], Brockenbrough, Davis); Scales's brigade under **Lowrance** + Lane, both under Trimble. Note: Lang's Floridians are *not* named in this passage. Rawley Martin adds Pickett's division regiment-by-regiment: Kemper right (1st, 3rd, 7th, 11th, 24th VA), Garnett left (8th, 18th, 19th, 28th, 56th VA), Armistead rear-center (9th, 14th, 38th, 53rd, 57th VA) — with the flag that some modern accounts place Armistead left-rear behind Garnett rather than Martin's "rear and center."

## Gap B — Four new verified voices (each with free full text + verbatim-checked quotes)

**Rawley W. Martin** (Lt. Col., 53rd VA, Armistead's brigade) + **John Holmes Smith** (Capt., 11th VA, Kemper's) — SHSP Vol. 32 (1904), pp. 183–195; Martin's letter dated Aug. 11, 1897. Full text: https://archive.org/stream/southernhistoricv32sout/southernhistoricv32sout_djvu.txt (mirror: gdg.org — expired TLS, use http). **The Armistead vignette:** hat on sword point, he "rushed forward, scaled the wall, and cried: 'Boys, give them the cold steel!'" and "had fallen, mortally wounded, under the guns he had captured." Martin: "I was disabled at Armistead's side a moment after he had fallen." Also corroborates pass-1 timing from inside the column: "About 1 o'clock two signal guns were fired by the Washington Artillery." Flags: 34-year recollection; twice misnames the Union position "Seminary Ridge."

**Birkett D. Fry** (Col., commanding Archer's brigade) — "Pettigrew's Charge at Gettysburg," SHSP Vol. 7 (1879), pp. 91–93. Full text: http://www.gdg.org/research/SHSP/shpettig.html (cite an archive.org SHSP v7 scan as backup). His brigade was the **guide of the whole assault** ("in the assault both divisions should allign themselves by it" — Garnett agreed to "dress on my command"). **Disagreement flag, encode it:** against Virginia-centric accounts (and Haskell), Fry insists Pettigrew's men fought to the wall — "All of the five regimental colors of my command reached the line of the enemy's works" — while Brockenbrough's brigade genuinely broke early; modern scholarship partially supports both. Caveats: Fry was down wounded before the end; misdates Heth's wounding.

**James Longstreet** — *From Manassas to Appomattox* (1896). Full texts: https://archive.org/details/manassasappomatt00longrich and Gutenberg #38418. The fifteen-thousand protest (pp. 386–387) is **indirect discourse in this memoir** — "Opinion was then expressed that the fifteen thousand men who could make successful assault over that field had never been arrayed for battle" — the famous direct-speech version comes from his earlier 1877 account; don't conflate. The order-refusal moment (p. 392): "Pickett said, 'General, shall I advance?' The effort to speak the order failed, and I could only indicate it by an affirmative bow." His reproduction of Alexander's 1:40 note differs slightly in wording from Alexander's own 1907 version — flag, don't reconcile.

**Ralph Orson Sturtevant** (13th Vermont, Stannard's brigade) — *Pictorial History Thirteenth Regiment Vermont Volunteers* (1910), pp. 304–305. Full text: https://archive.org/details/cu31924030916187. **The flank-attack vignette:** Stannard's "famous and now historic order (unique in manoeuvring in the midst of battle) 'Change front, forward on first company,'" after which each company "saw in their immediate front not sixty yards away General Pickett's command… and at once opened a deadly fire on their flank." Flag: the 1910 history ignores the known Stannard-vs-Doubleday/Hancock credit dispute.

**Formerly missing (2 of 6), now closed — see "Pass 3 addendum" below:** Frederick Fuger on Cushing's death (head-vs-mouth wound conflict with Haskell, now documented) and a Union enlisted bombardment voice (19th MA, Waitt 1906 — confirmed).

## Pass 3 addendum — final two voices (verified)

### 1. Frederick W. Fuger (1st Sgt., Battery A, 4th US Artillery — Cushing's battery) — VERIFIED, but only partially at the primary source

**Exact publication:** Frederick Fuger, "Cushing's Battery at Gettysburg," *Journal of the Military Service Institution of the United States*, Vol. 41 (Sept.–Oct. 1907), p. 408.

**Primary source access — could NOT be fetched free:** JMSI Vol. 41 (1907) is **not** on archive.org — the free digitized run of JMSI there stops at Vol. 39 (Nov–Dec 1906) and resumes at Vol. 42 (Jan–June 1908; https://archive.org/details/journalofmilitar4219mili) and Vol. 43 (July–Dec 1908). HathiTrust holds the catalog record (https://catalog.hathitrust.org/Record/008898037) but Cloudflare-blocks scripted fetches (403), so the volume could not be opened directly. **Flag this as an open item** — Vol. 41 exists in libraries/HathiTrust under login but has no verified free-and-scriptable full text.

**What IS verified — two independent routes converge on the same wording:**

1. **NPS Gettysburg National Military Park staff blog** (historian D. Scott Hartwig), fetched directly — https://npsgnmp.wordpress.com/2012/06/21/lieutenant-cushing/. This post cites the JMSI 1907 article by volume/page (above) and reproduces one passage as a **direct quote from the article itself**:
   > "This placed me in command of the Battery, and I shouted to the men to obey my orders. We continued to fire double and treble charges of our canister, but owing to the dense smoke, could not see very far to the front. At this moment to my utter amazement, I saw General Armistead leap over the stone wall with a number of his troops, landing right in the middle of our Battery. I shouted to my devoted cannoneers and drivers, who had no longer any horses, to stand their ground, which they heroically did, fighting hand to hand with hand spikes, pistols, sabers, ramrods and with help of Webb's Pennsylvania Brigade and that gallant Brigade of Vermonters commanded by that gallant General Stannard coming up our left flank; Pickett's charge collapsed."
   
   This is the **final-canister-moments passage**, confirmed verbatim against the 1907 JMSI text via the NPS citation (two independent fetches — mine and a sub-agent's — returned identical wording).

2. **Fuger's 1904 family-published manuscript** (a separate, earlier document — *not* the JMSI article, but overlapping heavily in wording, suggesting Fuger reused a standard narrative), titled *Lieut.-Colonel Frederick Fuger, U.S. Army and his Descendants* (Press of Winn & Hammond, Detroit, 1904), transcribed at https://storiesfrommimi.wordpress.com/descendants-of-lt-col-frederick-fuger/frederick-fuger-eyewitness-account-of-the-battle-of-the-bloody-angle-july-3-1863-gettysburg-pa/ (fetched directly). This gives the fuller **wound/death passage**, with the disputed detail:
   > "At this time, Cushing was wounded in the right shoulder, and in a few seconds in the abdomen, a terribly severe and painful wound. He called out, 'Fuger stand by me and impart my orders to the Battery' but he soon became faint and suffered frightfully. I wanted to have him taken to the rear, but he refused... When the enemy was within 200 yards, double and treble charges were fired, opening immense gaps in their lines... Lieutenant Miller, commanding the right half of the Battery, was at this juncture killed. **When the enemy had approached within 100 yards, Lieutenant Cushing was shot in the mouth and instantly killed.** I was standing on his right and a little in advance of him, when I saw him fall forwards; I caught him in my arms, and ordered two men to carry him to the rear."

**The head-vs-mouth conflict, documented:** Fuger's account is explicit and specific — "shot in the mouth" — as opposed to Haskell's "shot through the head" (pass-1/pass-2 citation). The NPS blog corroborates that this "shot in the mouth" framing is the one that made it into the 1907 JMSI article too (Hartwig paraphrases Fuger's JMSI sequence — shoulder wound, then abdomen wound, then the fatal shot — consistent with the 1904 manuscript above). Both wounds can be true in the sense that mouth-entry could still be described loosely by another witness as "through the head"; the sources are not reconcilable at the word level and should be presented as competing eyewitness details, not resolved.

**Caveats/biases (important, and worth encoding in the atlas):**
- Fuger's accounts were written **~41–44 years after the event**, during and after his pursuit of the Medal of Honor (awarded 1897) for manning Cushing's last gun — a direct personal stake in dramatizing his own and Cushing's final moments.
- NPS historian Hartwig's direct judgment (fetched verbatim): Fuger's claim that Cushing was disabled and needed to be held up at the wall **conflicts** with Capt. Andrew Cowan's Dec. 2, 1885 letter to Bachelder (Cushing gave a "pleasant reply" and continued shouting orders) and with Gen. Webb's official report (Cushing "fought his guns... for an hour and a half after he had reported to me that he was wounded in both thighs") — i.e., other testimony has Cushing wounded much earlier and functioning far longer than Fuger's narrative implies.
- Fuger's claim of personally fighting hand-to-hand at the gun after Armistead's men crossed the wall is **directly disputed** by Anthony McDermott (Co. I, 69th PA, 1891 testimony): "He is certainly mistaken... How could he stand there alive, when none of us were there, with that gun, firing it?"
- Hartwig's summary judgment (direct quote): "Fuger's embellishments to Cushing's tragic story gained traction over the years, largely because no one questioned the sergeant's account."
- **Net assessment:** usable and citable, but flag as a contested, self-interested retelling — not a neutral eyewitness. The canister/Armistead passage is the most solidly verified (matches the actual 1907 journal wording via NPS's direct quote); the wound/death passage is verified only via the 1904 manuscript variant, not the JMSI text itself, though the same "shot in the mouth" detail is corroborated as present in the JMSI account by Hartwig's paraphrase.

### 2. Union enlisted bombardment voice — VERIFIED: 19th Massachusetts (Waitt, 1906)

**Exact publication:** Ernest Linden Waitt (compiler), *History of the Nineteenth Regiment, Massachusetts Volunteer Infantry, 1861–1865* (Salem, Mass.: Salem Press Co., 1906), issued by the regiment's History Committee. Chapter XXIX, "Gettysburg, July 3, 1863," pp. 234–237.

**Free full-text URL (fetched directly, full djvu.txt OCR, ~29,500 lines):** https://archive.org/stream/04390879.3094.emory.edu/04390879_3094_djvu.txt (landing page: https://archive.org/details/04390879.3094.emory.edu)

**Verbatim passages, located and confirmed in the fetched text:**

> "Just at one o'clock the sharp report of a shotted gun within the enemy's lines, broke the oppressive stillness. It was plainly a signal. In an instant a round shot came bounding diagonally over the ridge, like a rubber ball... The report from the second gun had not died away before another shot came over the ridge, striking among the gun stacks of the Nineteenth Massachusetts, and then every rebel gun on Seminary Ridge opened in one grand salvo, with concentric fire on Gibbon's Division. From this time on, for an hour and thirty minutes, the roaring of cannon and the bursting of shells from both sides was so incessant that the ear could not distinguish individual explosions. It was one grand raging clash of ceaseless sound, — the most terrific cannonading of the war. The woods in front seemed lined with flame and smoke. Pandemonium broken loose was zephyr to a cyclone in comparison.
>
> Each man fell prostrate upon the level summit of the flat, low ridge. Just in front of the Nineteenth's line, the summit swelled perhaps two feet above their backs. From the ridge on which they lay, a hundred guns joined their clamor to the awful din... Rorty's battery was in a position in front of the regiment. Five horses and the drivers of the leading gun fell among the prostrate infantry men. Soon the third gun rolled helpless from its wheels. With but two guns left, heroic Rorty continued to fire. Then a caisson burst. Immediately his left piece was struck and shattered..."
>
> "The rain of shot and shell was continuous. Fragments of bursting shell were flying everywhere. There seemed to be no place where they did not strike and no spot from whence they did not come... So thick did the missiles fly that in a few moments nearly all of the inverted muskets were knocked down or shot off; pieces of shell were plainly visible as they hissed by; limber boxes and caissons were hit and blew up with stunning reports; the battery horses were nearly all shot down.
>
> Men, lying flat behind large boulders, were struck. A shot would come over and strike a man in the back. Men were constantly seen hobbling off, with blood streaming from their wounds. During all this time, not a musket had been fired at the enemy."

A named enlisted voice also survives as direct quoted dialogue within the same chapter — 2nd Lt. Moses Shackley (Co. B, "won his spurs in the ranks"), returning from the spring under fire: **"The water is cold enough, boys," he shouted, "but its devilish hot around the spring."**

**Rank/voice:** The chapter is narrated in the compiler's third-person composite voice (Waitt, writing in 1906 for the regiment's History Committee), not a single soldier's signed diary/letter excerpt — but it names and quotes specific enlisted men's actions within the bombardment (Sgt. "Billy" McGinnis, Sgts. Cornelius Linnehan and Matthias Bixby of Co. F, "Snellen, the sailor soldier from Marblehead," Lt. Shackley). No stronger single-signed private/sergeant diary passage for the July 3 cannonade specifically was found in this text (the book does contain a clearly-attributed sergeant's diary — Joseph E. Hodgkins — elsewhere, but that surviving excerpt covers his 1864 prison-camp experience, not July 3, 1863).

**Caveats:** Compiled 1906 regimental history, not a raw contemporaneous diary — expect 43-years-later literary polish ("Pandemonium broken loose was zephyr to a cyclone") layered over veterans' testimony. Still, it is specific, named, and vivid (unit, battery, individual casualties identified), and stands as a strong composite enlisted-ranks bombardment voice for Cemetery Ridge July 3.

**Unexplored fallback, not needed but flagged for later:** Holcombe, R.I., *History of the First Regiment Minnesota Volunteer Infantry, 1861–1864* (Stillwater, Minn.: Easton & Masterman, 1916) — free full text at https://archive.org/details/04090632.3107.emory.edu — located but not fetched, since Waitt already met the brief. Good candidate for a second/comparison bombardment voice if the atlas wants more than one.
