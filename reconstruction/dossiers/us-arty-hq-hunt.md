## EC1 — Identity & command

- Brig. Gen. Henry J. Hunt, Chief of Artillery, Army of the Potomac
  ("HENRY J. HUNT, Brigadier-General and Chief of Artillery,
  Commanding", or-27-1-hunt, report dated Sept. 27, 1863); command
  authority re-recognized July 2: "I considered this … as a
  recognition, for the present, at least, of the position I had held
  at Antietam and Fredericksburg, as commander of the artillery of the
  army" (or-27-1-hunt pp. 232-233, via the Barnett seminar PDF's
  page-cited quote).
- Materiel under command: "for the command of 67 batteries (372 guns),
  with over 8,000 men and 7,000 horses"; of the army's 320 field guns:
  "142 were light 12-pounders, 106 3-inch guns, 6 20-pounders, 60
  10-pounder Parrott guns, and a battery of 4 James rifles and 2
  12-pounder howitzers" + 44 horse-artillery 3-inch (or-27-1-hunt).
- **Authority conflict (first-class)**: on the II Corps front the
  batteries fired through the cannonade "under the orders of their
  corps commander" — Hunt's own indictment: "They had unfortunately
  exhausted their long range projectiles during the cannonade, under
  the orders of their corps commander, and it was too late to replace
  them. Had my instructions been followed here, as they were by
  McGilvery, I do not believe that Pickett's division would have
  reached our line" (hunt-bl-3, both reproductions). Hancock is NOT
  named in the verified sentence — the dispute is real, the naming is
  historiography; recorded precisely so.

## EC5 — Activity record (the orders as events)

1. **The 10:00 instruction** [C]: "I gave instructions to the
  batteries and to the chiefs of artillery not to fire at small
  bodies, nor to allow their fire to be drawn without promise of
  adequate results; to watch the enemy closely, and when he opened to
  concentrate the fire of their guns on one battery at a time until
  it was silenced; under all circumstances to fire deliberately, and
  to husband their ammunition as much as possible" (or-27-1-hunt).
  B&L adds the withhold interval: "to withhold their fire for fifteen
  or twenty minutes after the cannonade commenced" (hunt-bl-3) — and
  his inference of the enemy's purpose: "to exhaust our ammunition in
  reply, so that the assaulting troops might pass in good condition
  over the half mile of open ground which was beyond our effective
  musketry fire."
2. **The opening reply** [C]: "the enemy opened, at about 1 p. m. …
  from one hundred to one hundred and twenty" guns vs "more than
  eighty to reply effectively. Our fire was well withheld until the
  first burst was over … It was then opened deliberately and with
  excellent effect" (or-27-1-hunt).
3. **The cease order — CA-J3A-4's event, now clock-fixed by its
  author** [C, stated 14:30]: "About 2.30 p. m., finding our
  ammunition running low and that it was very unsafe to bring up
  loads of it, a number of caissons and limbers having been exploded,
  I directed that the fire should be gradually stopped, which was
  done, **and the enemy soon slackened his fire also**"
  (or-27-1-hunt). The B&L narrative adds the Meade-seeking ride, the
  Howard concurrence, and the convergence with Meade's own wish
  ("General Meade's aides were seeking me with orders to 'cease
  firing'; so I had only anticipated his wishes", via Maj. Bingham).
  Contemporary motive: ammunition + caisson losses; the lure effect
  is stated as consequence ("This was followed by a cessation of that
  of the enemy, under the mistaken impression that he had silenced
  our guns") — the RUSE framing strengthens in the postwar accounts
  (see us-xi-arty-osborn Conflicts).
4. **The assault** [C]: "About 3 p. m., and soon after the enemy's
  fire had ceased, he formed a column of attack in the edge of the
  woods in front of the Second Corps" (or-27-1-hunt); the McGilvery
  flanking directive (EC3.4); the II Corps canister-only constraint
  ("having nearly exhausted their supply of ammunition, except
  canister").
5. **Post-repulse replacements** [D]: "Toward the close of this
  struggle, Rorty's…, Arnold's…, and Cushing's… batteries … were
  withdrawn, and as soon as the affair was over their places were
  filled with fresh ones" (or-27-1-hunt).
6. **The ammunition arithmetic** [B inputs, now primary]: three-day
  expenditure "32,781 rounds, averaging over 100 rounds per gun" of
  "270 rounds per gun" carried — "left sufficient to fill the
  ammunition chests and enable the army to fight another battle"
  (or-27-1-hunt); the scare he blames on "large and unreasonable
  demands made by corps commanders who had left their own trains …
  behind".
